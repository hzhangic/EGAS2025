Motivation

In an era where algorithms shape critical decisions — from public services to urban planning — geospatial technologies play a key role in how we perceive and interact with the world. The increasing integration of AI into GIS platforms (GeoAI) enhances efficiency and capability but introduces new ethical risks. These include unintended biases in spatial data, misuse of predictive algorithms, surveillance concerns, and the erosion of privacy rights. As GIS professionals are tasked with managing vast amounts of location-based data, ensuring that these technologies contribute to a fair, accountable, and transparent society is paramount. This workshop draws inspiration from the Responsible AI principles in computer science, which seeks to advance efforts to make automated systems more just and equitable. By focusing specifically on the spatial context, this workshop contributes to the broader conversation on the responsible development and deployment of algorithms in society.

Broad Objective

Our long-term goal is to cultivate interdisciplinary collaboration across fields such as computer science, informatics, geography, and communication studies to advance both research and education in ethical GIS. We welcome qualitative and quantitative researchers, educators, and students engaged in geospatial data collection and ethical inquiry. Together, we aim to identify best practices for responsibly navigating the intersection of geospatial intelligence, algorithms, and social impact.

Keynote

Speaker: Luis F. Alvarez León
Associate Professor of Geography
Dartmouth College

Title: The Map in the Machine: Charting the Spatial Architecture of Digital Capitalism

Abstract: Digital technologies have changed how we shop, work, play, and communicate, reshaping our societies and economies. To understand digital capitalism, we need to grasp how advances in geospatial technologies underpin the construction, operation, and refinement of markets for digital goods and services. In The Map in the Machine, Luis F. Alvarez Leon examines these advances, from MapQuest and Google Maps to the rise of IP geolocation, ridesharing, and a new Earth Observation satellite ecosystem. He develops a geographical theory of digital capitalism centered on the processes of location, valuation, and marketization to provide a new vantage point from which to better understand, and intervene in, the dominant techno-economic paradigm of our time. By centering the spatiality of digital capitalism, Alvarez Leon shows how this system is the product not of seemingly intangible information clouds but rather of a vast array of technologies, practices, and infrastructures deeply rooted in place, mediated by geography, and open to contestation and change.

Biography: Luis F. Alvarez Leon is Associate Professor of Geography at Dartmouth College. He researches the political economy of geospatial data, media, and technologies.
